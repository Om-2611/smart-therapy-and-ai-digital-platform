Understanding Specific Learning Disabilities (SLD)

Specific Learning Disabilities are neurological conditions
that affect the brain's ability to receive, process, store,
and respond to information. Children with SLD have average
or above-average intelligence but struggle with reading,
writing, or mathematical skills.

Dyslexia is the most common SLD, affecting approximately
15-20% of the population. It is characterised by difficulties
with accurate and fluent word recognition, poor spelling, and
decoding abilities. Children with dyslexia often struggle to
connect letters with their sounds.

Therapeutic approaches for dyslexia include multisensory
structured language instruction, phonics-based interventions,
and the Orton-Gillingham method. These approaches use visual,
auditory, and kinesthetic pathways simultaneously to reinforce
learning.

Dyscalculia affects mathematical processing. Children may
struggle to understand number concepts, learn number facts,
and perform accurate math calculations. Interventions include
concrete manipulatives, visual representations, and building
number sense through games and activities.

Working memory deficits are common in SLD and ADHD. Children
may forget instructions mid-task, lose track of where they
are in a multi-step problem, or have difficulty holding
information while processing other information.

Therapeutic module recommendations for SLD clients:
- Word Building activities strengthen phonemic awareness
- Bubble Splash reinforces sight word recognition
- Whack-a-Mole Math builds number fact fluency
- Pixel Art Coding develops sequential thinking
- Digital Sand Tray supports emotional processing when
  learning frustration is high
